The Akkusativ Case
Objects in German

The Akkusativ is the first case change you encounter in German. It affects the article in front of a noun when that noun is the object of a verb. Only one article actually changes — but getting it right or wrong is the difference between a correct and incorrect sentence every time you use a transitive verb.

The good news: Only the masculine article changes in the Akkusativ. der → den. die stays die. das stays das. Learn one change and you know the Akkusativ.

Subject vs Object — why the article changes

In German, the article in front of a noun changes to show its role in the sentence. The Nominativ (subject) is who is doing the action. The Akkusativ (direct object) is what is being acted upon.

Der Mann  kauft  einen Kaffee.
SUBJECT (Nominativ)
who is buying
VERB
the action
OBJECT (Akkusativ)
what is bought

In English, word order tells you who is doing what. "The dog bites the man" vs "The man bites the dog" — same words, different meaning, because of position. In German, cases carry that information. So German can rearrange words for emphasis — the case endings signal the role, not the position.

Den Mann  beißt  der Hund.
OBJECT (Akkusativ)
being bitten
VERB
SUBJECT (Nominativ)
doing the biting
The key insight: "Den Mann beißt der Hund" = "The man is bitten by the dog." The word order changed for emphasis — but "den" signals that Mann is still the object, not the subject. Without cases, this rearrangement would be impossible. Cases give German its word-order flexibility.
The change you must know:

der (masculine Nominativ) → den (masculine Akkusativ)
die (feminine) → stays die
das (neuter) → stays das
die (plural) → stays die

The whole Akkusativ in one sentence: Masculine articles gain an -n. der → den. ein → einen. kein → keinen. mein → meinen. That -n ending on the masculine object is the entire Akkusativ case change at A1 level. Everything else is identical to the Nominativ.

Negation — kein and nicht

German has two ways to say "no/not" — kein and nicht. Choosing the wrong one is one of the most common A1 mistakes. The rule is simple once you see it.

KEIN — use for nouns
Negates a noun.
Replaces ein/eine/ein.
Also negates nouns with no article.

Ich habe kein Geld.
Ich habe keine Zeit.
Ich habe keinen Bruder.
NICHT — use for everything else
Negates a verb, adjective, or specific noun with definite article.

Ich arbeite nicht.
Das ist nicht gut.
Ich nehme nicht den Bus.
kein in Akkusativ — it changes too

kein follows the same pattern as ein/eine/ein — so in the Akkusativ, masculine kein adds -en just like ein → einen.

GenderNominativAkkusativExample
Masculine kein keinen Ich habe keinen Bruder.
Feminine keine keine Ich habe keine Zeit.
Neuter kein kein Ich habe kein Geld.
Plural keine keine Ich habe keine Kinder.
Negation in real sentences — tap to hear
KEIN
Ich habe keinen Hunger.
I am not hungry. (literally: I have no hunger.)
Hunger is a masculine noun (der Hunger) → keinen in Akkusativ. "Ich habe nicht Hunger" is wrong — nouns take kein, not nicht.
NICHT
Ich arbeite heute nicht.
I am not working today.
Negating a verb (arbeiten) → nicht. Position: nicht goes after the verb and all complements, usually near the end.
KEIN
Wir haben keine Zeit.
We have no time.
Zeit is feminine (die Zeit) → keine in Akkusativ. Feminine kein stays keine — same in Nominativ and Akkusativ.
NICHT
Das ist nicht richtig.
That is not correct.
Negating an adjective (richtig) → nicht. "Das ist kein richtig" is completely wrong — kein only works before nouns.

Akkusativ prepositions — always den, never der

Certain prepositions always take the Akkusativ. This means the noun after them always uses the Akkusativ article — even if it would otherwise be the subject of a sentence. Memorise this list. It is short and fixed.

The five Akkusativ prepositions — memory trick:

durch · für · gegen · ohne · um

Memory: durch für gegen ohne um → "dfgou" or just practise each one with an example sentence.
DURCH
Wir fahren durch den Tunnel.
We drive through the tunnel.
durch = through. Tunnel is masculine (der Tunnel) → durch den Tunnel (Akkusativ).
FÜR
Das ist für einen Freund.
That is for a friend.
für = for. Freund is masculine (der Freund) → für einen Freund (Akkusativ). "für ein Freund" is wrong.
GEGEN
Ich bin gegen den Plan.
I am against the plan.
gegen = against. Plan is masculine (der Plan) → gegen den Plan.
OHNE
Ich trinke Kaffee ohne Zucker.
I drink coffee without sugar.
ohne = without. Zucker is masculine (der Zucker) → ohne den Zucker or ohne Zucker (no article when speaking generally).
UM
Wir treffen uns um drei Uhr.
We meet at three o'clock.
um = at (time) / around. "um drei Uhr" is the standard time expression. Also: um die Ecke (around the corner).
Für is the most common in Goethe: "Das ist für Sie" (That is for you — formal), "ein Kurs für Anfänger" (a course for beginners), "für wie lange?" (for how long?). It appears in nearly every Goethe section. Always triggers Akkusativ after it.

Akkusativ in real Goethe sentences

The Akkusativ appears in every section of the Goethe exam. These are the real sentence patterns the examiner uses. Read, hear, and internalise each one.

Goethe Sprechen — introduction sentences
AKK
Ich habe einen Bruder und eine Schwester.
I have a brother and a sister.
haben takes Akkusativ. Bruder (m) → einen. Schwester (f) → eine (no change for feminine).
AKK
Ich suche einen Kurs für Anfänger.
I am looking for a beginner course.
suchen takes Akkusativ. Kurs (m) → einen Kurs. für also takes Akkusativ: für Anfänger.
Goethe Hören — sentences to recognise
AKK
Nehmen Sie den Bus Nummer zwölf.
Take bus number twelve.
nehmen takes Akkusativ. Bus (m) → den Bus. This appears in Goethe Hören transport directions.
AKK
Bitte füllen Sie den Antrag vollständig aus.
Please fill in the form completely.
ausfüllen takes Akkusativ. Antrag (m) → den Antrag. Common in Goethe Hören office and administration contexts.
Goethe Schreiben — sentences you write
AKK
Ich möchte einen Termin machen.
I would like to make an appointment.
möchte (would like) takes Akkusativ on its object. Termin (m) → einen Termin. The standard phrase for booking appointments — appears in Schreiben task 2.
AKK
Ich brauche keinen Kaffee, danke.
I don't need any coffee, thank you.
brauchen takes Akkusativ. Kaffee (m) → keinen Kaffee (negated with kein in Akkusativ).
AKK
Das Geschenk ist für meinen Vater.
The gift is for my father.
für takes Akkusativ. mein (possessive) also adds -en for masculine Akkusativ: meinen Vater.
What to listen for in Goethe Hören: When you hear "den" before a masculine noun, you know the noun is an object. This is a comprehension clue. "Nehmen Sie den Bus" — you immediately know the bus is what you should take (object), not a new subject. Cases help you understand sentences faster.
