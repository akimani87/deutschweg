The Dativ Case
The Indirect Object

The Dativ is the third case. Where the Akkusativ marked the direct object (what is acted on), the Dativ marks the indirect object — the recipient, the beneficiary, the person something is done for or given to. It also appears after a fixed set of prepositions.

The Dativ changes all genders: Unlike Akkusativ (only masculine changes), the Dativ changes masculine, feminine, and neuter. dem · der · dem · den — learn these four forms and you have the Dativ.

The indirect object — to whom, for whom

The Dativ marks the indirect object — the person or thing that receives the direct object. The simplest test: ask "to whom?" or "for whom?" after the verb. That answer takes the Dativ.

Ich  gebe  dem Mann  einen Kaffee.
SUBJECT
Nominativ
VERB
geben
INDIRECT OBJ
Dativ — to whom?
DIRECT OBJ
Akkusativ — what?

Notice: the sentence has three noun roles at once. "Ich" is the subject (Nominativ). "Einen Kaffee" is what is given (Akkusativ). "Dem Mann" is the recipient — to whom it is given (Dativ). German handles all three with different articles.

The three questions to identify cases:

Who does it? → Nominativ (subject)
What is done? → Akkusativ (direct object)
To/for whom? → Dativ (indirect object)
More examples — tap to hear
DATIV
Ich schreibe meiner Mutter eine E-Mail.
I am writing my mother an email.
To whom do I write? → meiner Mutter (Dativ, feminine). What do I write? → eine E-Mail (Akkusativ).
DATIV
Er zeigt dem Arzt seinen Arm.
He shows the doctor his arm.
To whom does he show? → dem Arzt (Dativ, masculine). What does he show? → seinen Arm (Akkusativ).
DATIV
Kannst du mir bitte helfen?
Can you help me, please?
"mir" is the Dativ of "ich" — the personal pronoun form. helfen always takes Dativ: helfen + wem? (Lesson 3 covers Dativ pronouns and helfen fully).

The feminine Dativ trap: Feminine Dativ is der — the same spelling as masculine Nominativ. "Ich helfe der Frau" — "der" here is NOT masculine Nominativ. It is feminine Dativ. Context (the verb helfen, the fact that Frau is feminine) tells you which case it is. This is where students most commonly confuse forms.
The Dativ memory pattern:

Definite: dem · der · dem · den
Indefinite: einem · einer · einem · —

Masculine and neuter share the same Dativ form (dem/einem). Feminine is different (der/einer). Plural definite adds -n to the noun as well as changing to den.

Verbs that always take the Dativ

Most verbs that take an object take the Akkusativ. But a specific group of verbs always takes the Dativ — even though they seem like normal action verbs. These must be memorised. They appear constantly in Goethe.

The most important Dativ verb: helfen (to help) always takes Dativ. "Ich helfe dem Mann" — NOT "Ich helfe den Mann." In English "help someone" takes a direct object. In German it takes Dativ. This is the verb students most often get wrong.
helfen
Kannst du mir helfen?
Can you help me?
helfen + Dativ always. "mir" = Dativ of ich. "Kannst du mich helfen?" is incorrect — mich is Akkusativ.
gefallen
Das Essen gefällt mir sehr gut.
I like the food very much. (literally: The food pleases me.)
gefallen = to please / to like (reversed structure). What pleases (the food) is the Nominativ subject. The person who is pleased (me) is Dativ. "Ich gefalle das Essen" is completely wrong.
danken
Ich danke Ihnen sehr.
I thank you very much. (formal)
danken + Dativ. "Ihnen" = formal Dativ of Sie. Used in formal written and spoken German. Appears in Goethe Schreiben letter endings.
gehören
Das Buch gehört meiner Schwester.
The book belongs to my sister.
gehören + Dativ. meiner Schwester = feminine Dativ (eine → einer → meiner). "belongs to" = gehört + Dativ person.
antworten
Ich antworte dem Lehrer.
I answer the teacher.
antworten + Dativ. dem Lehrer = masculine Dativ. "Ich antworte den Lehrer" is wrong (Akkusativ). This verb consistently catches students.
es geht
Wie geht es Ihnen?
How are you? (formal)
"Wie geht es Ihnen?" — you already know this phrase. Now you know why: "Ihnen" is formal Dativ. "Wie geht es mir?" = How am I doing? The fixed expression always uses Dativ.

Dativ prepositions — always dem, never den

These prepositions always take the Dativ — no exceptions, no context dependency. After any of these prepositions, every noun takes the Dativ article. Tap each to hear an example sentence.

aus
from / out of

🔊 tap

at / with (someone's place)
Ich bin bei meiner Mutter.
🔊 tap
mit
with / by (transport)
Ich fahre mit dem Bus.
🔊 tap
nach
to (cities/countries) / after
Ich fahre nach Berlin.
🔊 tap
seit
since / for (ongoing)
Ich lerne seit drei Jahren.
🔊 tap
von
from / of / by
Das ist von meinem Chef.
🔊 tap

to (a person/place)
Ich gehe zum Arzt.
🔊 tap
gegenüber
opposite / across from
gegenüber dem Bahnhof
🔊 tap
Three contractions you must know:

zu + dem → zum  (Ich gehe zum Arzt.)
zu + der → zur  (Ich gehe zur Schule.)
von + dem → vom  (Das ist vom Chef.)

These contractions are mandatory in natural German — "zu dem" sounds unnatural and marks you as a learner. The exam expects zum/zur/vom.
seit — a crucial Goethe preposition: seit means "since" or "for" and describes something that started in the past and is STILL happening now. It always uses the present tense in German, not the past.

Ich lerne seit zwei Jahren Deutsch.
I have been learning German for two years. (and I still am)

NOT: "Ich habe seit zwei Jahren Deutsch gelernt." That implies it is finished.

Two-way prepositions — movement or location?

Nine prepositions can take either Akkusativ or Dativ depending on meaning. The rule is logical and consistent: Akkusativ signals movement toward a destination. Dativ signals location — being somewhere.

AKKUSATIV
Movement → destination
Ask: "where to?" (wohin?)
Ich lege das Buch in die Tasche.
I put the book into the bag.

Er geht in die Schule.
He goes to school.
DATIV
Location — being there
Ask: "where?" (wo?)
Das Buch liegt in der Tasche.
The book is in the bag.

Er ist in der Schule.
He is at school.
The nine two-way prepositions
in an auf über unter vor hinter neben zwischen
The key pairs — hear the difference
WOHIN?
Ich gehe in die Küche.
I am going into the kitchen. (movement)
Wohin? → in die Küche (Akkusativ, feminine die unchanged).
WO?
Ich bin in der Küche.
I am in the kitchen. (location)
Wo? → in der Küche (Dativ, feminine die → der).
WOHIN?
Er fährt in den Bahnhof.
He drives into the station. (movement)
Wohin? → in den Bahnhof (Akkusativ, masculine der → den).
WO?
Er ist im Bahnhof.
He is in the station. (location)
Wo? → im Bahnhof (in + dem = im, masculine Dativ contraction).
Two more contractions:

in + dem → im  (im Bahnhof, im Supermarkt, im Krankenhaus)
an + dem → am  (am Montag, am Bahnhof, am Wochenende)

"im" and "am" are mandatory contractions in natural German and appear constantly in Goethe Hören.

Dativ in real Goethe sentences

These are the sentence patterns where the Dativ actually appears in the exam. Read, hear, and understand why each Dativ form is correct.

Goethe Sprechen — conversation sentences
DATIV
Ich fahre mit dem Zug nach Berlin.
I travel to Berlin by train.
mit takes Dativ. Zug is masculine → mit dem Zug. nach takes Dativ. Berlin has no article (most cities) → nach Berlin.
DATIV
Ich wohne bei meiner Schwester.
I live with my sister.
bei takes Dativ. Schwester is feminine → bei meiner Schwester (feminine Dativ: meine → meiner).
Goethe Hören — directions and instructions
DATIV
Die Apotheke ist gegenüber dem Bahnhof.
The pharmacy is opposite the train station.
gegenüber takes Dativ. Bahnhof is masculine → gegenüber dem Bahnhof. Classic Goethe Hören directions.
DATIV
Der Kurs beginnt am Dienstag im Stadtpark.
The course begins on Tuesday in the city park.
am = an + dem (Dativ). im = in + dem (Dativ). Both mandatory contractions in natural speech.
Goethe Schreiben — sentences you produce
DATIV
Ich lerne seit einem Jahr Deutsch.
I have been learning German for one year.
seit takes Dativ. Jahr is neuter (das Jahr) → seit einem Jahr (neuter Dativ: ein → einem). The present tense with seit shows ongoing action.
DATIV
Können Sie mir bitte helfen?
Can you please help me? (formal)
helfen always takes Dativ. "mir" = Dativ of ich. Formal with Sie. This exact sentence appears in Goethe Sprechen role-play tasks.
DATIV
Das Geschäft ist vom Montag bis zum Freitag geöffnet.
The shop is open from Monday to Friday.
vom = von + dem. zum = zu + dem. Both Dativ contractions. This sentence structure appears in every Goethe Lesen opening-hours text.
Your complete case system so far:

Nominativ — who does it (subject)
Akkusativ — what is done (direct object / durch·für·gegen·ohne·um)
Dativ — to/for whom (indirect object / aus·bei·mit·nach·seit·von·zu)

Three cases. Three article sets. One logical system. You now have the grammar core of A1 and A2 German.
