Negation:
nicht and kein

English has one word for "not" — and it goes right after the verb. German has two completely different negation words, each with its own rules, and neither of them works exactly like English "not." This module gives you the decision logic, the grammar, the word order rules, and the exam strategy — so that negation becomes free points in your Goethe A1 Schreiben.

The core question of German negation: Is what you're negating a noun with an indefinite or no article? → Use kein. Is it a verb, adjective, adverb, or specific named thing? → Use nicht. Two words. One question. That is the whole system.

nicht vs kein — the core rule

English speakers learning German face an immediate problem: English has one negation word — "not" — and it goes in one predictable place (right after "be" or with "do"). German has two negation words that work differently from each other and differently from English "not." The good news: the decision between them follows one clear rule.

nicht
NEGATES EVERYTHING ELSE
Use nicht to negate:
· verbs — Ich arbeite nicht
· adjectives — Er ist nicht müde
· adverbs — nicht heute
· proper nouns — Das ist nicht Kwame
kein
NEGATES NOUNS
Use kein to negate:
· nouns with ein/eine — kein Auto
· nouns with no article — kein Geld
· nouns after sein/haben — kein Arzt
The one-question decision test
❓ Could the positive sentence use "ein" or "eine" before the noun — OR does the noun have no article at all?
YES → USE
kein
Ich habe ein Auto → Ich habe kein Auto
Ich habe Geld → Ich habe kein Geld
NO → USE
nicht
Ich arbeite → Ich arbeite nicht
Ich bin müde → Ich bin nicht müde

Note on "kein Geld": When a noun has no article in the positive sentence ("Ich habe Geld"), you still use kein to negate it — not nicht. Think of it as: kein replaces the missing article with a negative article. "Ich habe kein Geld" = "I have no money."

kein — forms, genders, cases

kein is not just one word — it changes form based on the gender of the noun it negates and the grammatical case. It follows the same pattern as ein/eine. If you know how ein changes, you already know how kein changes.

The highlighted cell is the only change: masculine accusative adds –en → keinen.

The key forms to know — with examples
MASC · ACC
Amina hat keinen Bruder.
Amina has no brother.
Bruder = masculine. After haben, the noun is in the accusative case. Masculine accusative → keinen. This is the most commonly tested form at A1.
NEUT · ACC
Kofi hat kein Auto.
Kofi has no car.
Auto = neuter. Neuter nominative and accusative are both kein — no change needed.
FEM · ACC
Fatima hat keine Schwester.
Fatima has no sister.
Schwester = feminine. Feminine accusative → keine — same as nominative.
PLURAL
Kwame hat keine Kinder.
Kwame has no children.
Kinder = plural. All plural forms → keine.
kein after sein — predicate nouns

When you negate a profession or identity after sein (to be), use kein — even though there is no article in the positive sentence. German treats profession nouns after sein as if they had an indefinite article.

SEIN + NOUN
Kwame ist Arzt. → Kwame ist kein Arzt.
Kwame is a doctor. → Kwame is not a doctor.
Positive: "Kwame ist Arzt" (no article). Negation: kein Arzt (not "nicht Arzt"). After sein, use kein to negate a noun — not nicht.
SEIN + NOUN
Fatima ist Lehrerin. → Fatima ist keine Lehrerin.
Fatima is a teacher. → Fatima is not a teacher.
Lehrerin = feminine → keine. Remember: feminine noun → keine (in both nominative and accusative).
The memory shortcut for kein forms:

Only one form is different from the others: keinen (masculine accusative — after haben with a masculine noun).
All other forms are either kein (masculine and neuter nominative/accusative) or keine (feminine + plural).

If unsure: ask yourself — is the noun masculine AND after haben? → keinen. Everything else → kein or keine.

nicht — what it negates

nicht is the general negation word. It negates everything that is not a noun with an indefinite or no article: verbs, predicative adjectives, adverbs, proper nouns, and also definite articles (the, this). Learn the categories and the position rule will follow naturally.

nicht + verb — negating the action
VERB
Kofi arbeitet nicht.
Kofi does not work.
Negating the verb → nicht at the end of the sentence.
VERB
Amina schläft nicht.
Amina is not sleeping.
Same pattern: verb + nicht at end.
nicht + sein — negating "to be" + adjective
SEIN + ADJ
Ich bin nicht müde.
I am not tired.
Predicative adjective after sein → nicht comes directly before the adjective.
SEIN + ADJ
Das Wetter ist nicht gut.
The weather is not good.
gut = adjective after sein → nicht before the adjective.
nicht + adverb — negating time, place, manner
ADVERB
Fatima kommt nicht heute.
Fatima is not coming today.
nicht before heute = specifically negating the time (not today — perhaps another day). Compare: "Fatima kommt nicht." (not coming at all).
ADVERB
Kwame wohnt nicht hier.
Kwame does not live here.
nicht before hier = negating the place specifically.
nicht + proper noun — "that is not X"
PROPER NOUN
Das ist nicht Amina.
That is not Amina.
Amina = proper noun (a name). Names never take kein — always nicht.
Summary — what nicht negates:

✓ Verbs → Ich arbeite nicht
✓ Predicative adjectives after sein → Ich bin nicht müde
✓ Adverbs of time / place / manner → nicht heute, nicht hier
✓ Proper nouns (names) → Das ist nicht Kwame
✓ Definite articles (the) → Ich kaufe nicht das Buch (I'm not buying that specific book)

Position of nicht in the sentence

The biggest nicht question after "which word?" is "where does it go?" Unlike English "not" which sits immediately after the auxiliary verb, German nicht moves around depending on what it is negating. There are three clear positions to learn.

Rule 1 — nicht at the end: whole-sentence negation

When you want to negate the entire sentence (the whole action or event), nicht goes to the end of the clause — after all other elements.

WHOLE SENTENCE NEGATION · nicht AT END
Ich arbeite nicht.
I do not work. (the whole action is negated)
WHOLE SENTENCE NEGATION · nicht AT END
Kofi kommt morgen nicht.
Kofi is not coming tomorrow. (nicht goes after morgen, still at end)

Rule 2 — nicht before adjective: sein + predicate

When the sentence has sein + a predicative adjective, nicht goes directly before that adjective. Because the adjective is already at the end, nicht ends up near the end too — but it comes before the adjective, not after.

IchSUBJECT
binSEIN
nichtNEG
müdeADJECTIVE
I am not tired. (nicht before the adjective)
Rule 3 — nicht before specific element: targeted negation

When you want to negate a specific word or phrase — not the whole sentence — nicht goes directly before the element it negates. Whatever word comes immediately after nicht is what is being denied.

NEGATING TIME · nicht before heute
Fatima kommt nicht heute.
Fatima is not coming today. (implies she may come another day)
NEGATING PLACE · nicht before hier
Kwame wohnt nicht hier.
Kwame does not live here. (he lives somewhere else)
NEGATING PREP PHRASE · nicht before in + place
Amina wohnt nicht in Berlin.
Amina does not live in Berlin. (she lives somewhere else)
The one position that is always wrong:

NEVER put nicht between the subject and the conjugated verb.

✗ "Ich nicht arbeite." — completely wrong
✗ "Kofi nicht kommt." — completely wrong

✓ Ich arbeite nicht. — correct
✓ Kofi kommt nicht. — correct

German verbs must stay in second position. nicht never separates subject and verb.
A1 position summary — the simple version:

1. Negating the whole sentence → nicht at the end
2. After sein + adjective → nicht before the adjective (near end)
3. Negating a specific word → nicht directly before that word
4. Never between subject and verb

The English trap and exam tips

English-speaking learners make two very predictable negation errors. The first is using nicht where kein is needed. The second is placing nicht in the English position — immediately after the verb — instead of near the end. Both errors appear in every A1 exam script from learners who have not studied this module.

Error 1 — using nicht instead of kein

English: "I do not have a car" → Word-for-word German: "Ich habe nicht ein Auto" — WRONG. When the positive sentence uses ein/eine, you must replace it with kein. You cannot keep the article and add nicht before it.

WRONG · nicht + ein
Ich habe nicht ein Auto.
Ich habe nicht eine Schwester.
Ich habe nicht ein Kind.
CORRECT · kein replaces ein
Ich habe kein Auto.
Ich habe keine Schwester.
Ich habe kein Kind.
Error 2 — using nicht instead of kein with no-article nouns
WRONG · nicht + noun
Ich habe nicht Geld.
Kwame hat nicht Zeit.
Ich trinke nicht Wasser.
CORRECT · kein + noun
Ich habe kein Geld.
Kwame hat keine Zeit.
Ich trinke kein Wasser.
Error 3 — placing nicht immediately after the verb
WRONG · English position
Ich bin nicht
Ich komme nicht
(these look right but check below)
CORRECT — wait, these ARE right
Ich bin nicht müde ✓
Ich komme nicht ✓
Ich bin nicht krank ✓

These are actually correct because sein + adjective puts nicht before the adjective (near the end), and "Ich komme nicht" has nicht at the very end. The only wrong pattern is subject + verb + nicht when the verb is NOT sein: "Ich arbeite nicht heute" ✓ (nicht before heute) vs "Ich nicht arbeite" ✗ (subject–verb split).

Exam tip — negation in Goethe A1 Schreiben
Why negation = free exam points:

Negation appears in every A1 Schreiben task. You will always need to say what you do not have, what you are not, or what you do not do. The grader is looking for two things:

1. Correct choice of kein vs nicht
2. Correct position of nicht in the sentence

If you get both right, it counts as correct grammar — multiple points per sentence. If you consistently use "nicht ein" instead of "kein", it shows a systematic gap and costs marks throughout.
